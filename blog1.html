Why Data Science in Social Contexts Matters

On ethics, responsibility, and what doing this kind of work carefully actually demands of us.

Something I Keep Coming Back To

There is a common assumption in data science that if you follow the right steps, clean the data, choose the right model, validate your results, the work speaks for itself. The numbers are neutral. The model just reflects what is there.

I started this program thinking that way. I do not think that way anymore.

Data never exists in a vacuum. It is always collected by someone, from someone, for some purpose. And those choices shape every conclusion that follows. As I have worked through these projects, I keep running into moments where a technically correct answer does not feel like a complete one.

What I have come to believe: Data science applied to social questions is not just a technical practice. It is an ethical one. The moment your model affects a real person's life, their healthcare access, their loan application, their school assignment, it carries moral weight whether you intended that or not.

What "Social Context" Actually Means

When I talk about social context, I mean cases where the data is about people and where the outputs are used to make decisions that affect those same people. Healthcare models, hiring filters, credit scores, school assignment algorithms. In every one of these cases, the model is not just describing the world. It is participating in it.

That is what makes this different from predicting equipment failure or optimizing a supply chain. When the output is a person being denied care or sorted into a less-resourced school, the stakes change entirely. I started thinking about this differently when I was working on the diabetes project. The 768 rows in that dataset are real patients. They did not consent to being part of a machine learning homework assignment decades later. That is something I try to keep in mind.

The Problem with Historical Data

Many predictive models are trained on historical data. The assumption baked in is that the past is a reasonable guide to the future. But if the historical data reflects a world with systematic inequities, and almost all social data does, then a model trained on that data will learn to replicate those inequities. It will predict that certain groups are higher risk not because they genuinely are, but because they were treated that way in the training data.

The Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset reflects a population with historically high rates of diabetes, driven in part by decades of displacement and food insecurity. A model trained on that data is not just predicting health outcomes. It is learning from a history of harm. That does not mean the model is useless, but it means we need to be honest about what we are working with.

The uncomfortable truth: A technically accurate model can still be a socially harmful one. Accuracy measured against historical outcomes is not the same as fairness. In social contexts, those two things are often in direct tension with each other.

What Interdisciplinary Work Actually Demands

Working responsibly in this space requires more than technical skill. It requires knowing enough about the context to understand where the data came from and what is at stake. It requires asking not just whether the model works, but who it works for and who it works against. It requires humility about the fact that your model is a simplification of something real, not the thing itself.

And sometimes it requires the willingness to say: this is technically feasible, but we should not build it. That is a harder conversation. But responsible data science has to make room for it.

Why I Think About This in Every Project

My projects so far have been small in scale. 300 records, 70 survey responses. But even at that scale, questions of representation, bias, and harm keep appearing. Who is in the dataset? Who is missing? What does it mean to impute a value, or to choose which features to include and which to leave out?

These are practice questions for the real ones. The habits I build now around how I think about data will carry into work that affects real people at real scale. That is the reason I take them seriously even when the dataset is small.

What I take away: The technical part of this work is learnable from a textbook. The ethical part requires something different. It requires seeing data not just as numbers, but as records of human lives, and taking that seriously at every step of the process.
