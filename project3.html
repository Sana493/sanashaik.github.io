Screen Time and Mental Health

Why This Project Matters to Me

Mental health is something I have personally seen become more talked about, but still not fully understood, especially among college students. At UNC Charlotte, I have noticed how common it is for students, including myself sometimes, to spend hours on social media or gaming either to relax or to escape from stress.

What stood out to me is that the impact of this usage is not the same for everyone. Some people feel better after using these platforms, while others feel more anxious, lonely, or drained. That difference is what made this project meaningful to me.

Research supports this complexity too. The Pew Research Center has reported that social media can increase pressure and anxiety among teens, while sources like Psychology Today highlight that online platforms can also provide connection and support. Reports summarized by Engadget indicate that around 37% of teens report sleep disruption from platforms like TikTok.

Our research question: How accurately can hours of social media and gaming usage, combined with self-reported psychological responses, predict the day-to-day mental health outcomes (daily mood) of individuals? We wanted to move beyond simple assumptions and use machine learning to detect real patterns in how behavior and psychological responses together relate to mood.

Why We Collected Our Own Data

My teammates Nik, Ojas, and I all had the same intuition going in. We did not think raw screen hours were the real story. We thought the why mattered more than the how long. We could not find an existing dataset that captured both behavioral screen habits and the psychological side-effects we cared about, so we built one ourselves.

We distributed a Google Form survey across UNC Charlotte and the broader Charlotte community in April 2026 and collected 100 responses. After cleaning, 70 were usable.

Exploring the Data

Before modeling anything, we spent time understanding what the data actually looked like. We ran several visualizations to find patterns and check how our key variables were distributed.

Mood Distribution

The first thing to check was how our target variable was distributed. Most respondents rated their mood at 2.0 or 3.0, with very few reporting extremely bad or good days. This imbalance matters because it means a naive model that always predicts "Neutral" would still look decent on paper.

Fig 1. Mood distribution across all 70 respondents. Neutral and Good moods dominate, with very few reporting Bad days. This class imbalance shaped how we evaluated our models.

Social Media Hours vs. Daily Mood

The relationship between social media usage and mood shows a generally downward trend. Respondents reporting moderate usage around 1 to 2 hours showed the lowest average mood, while those at the extremes varied more. The wide confidence interval tells us there is a lot of individual variation here, which is part of why a simple correlation is not enough.

Fig 2. Social media hours vs. daily mood. There is a rough downward trend but with significant spread, suggesting screen time alone is not the whole story.

Gaming Hours vs. Daily Mood

Gaming shows a clearer downward pattern. Respondents who reported higher daily gaming hours tended to report lower mood, with the confidence band widening at higher usage levels due to fewer data points. This is consistent with the avoidance coping pattern we found later in the feature importance analysis.

Fig 3. Gaming hours vs. daily mood. The negative trend is more pronounced at higher usage levels, though the sample is smaller at those extremes.

What We Measured

After cleaning, we had 70 usable rows and 11 features. The target variable was Daily Mood, rated 0 to 4 by each respondent and then binned into three classes.

  • Social Media Hours - daily time on social platforms, ordinal
  • Video Game Hours - daily gaming time, ordinal
  • Anxiety Levels - self-reported anxiety linked to screen use
  • Social Comparison - tendency to compare oneself to others online
  • Self Esteem - self-reported rating
  • Screen Use for Relief - whether screens are used to cope with stress
  • Loneliness - feelings of isolation linked to screen use
  • Sleep Affected - whether screen use disrupts sleep
  • Addiction Level - self-assessed compulsive use
  • Gamer Status - whether the respondent identifies as a gamer

Target variable: Daily Mood rated 0 to 4, binned as Bad (0 to 1), Neutral (2), and Good (3 to 4).

Worth naming upfront: With 70 rows and 14 test samples, every percentage point of accuracy represents about 0.14 samples. We treated our results as directional signals rather than definitive conclusions, and tried to be honest about that throughout.

The Five Models We Trained

Support Vector Machine

RBF kernel with class_weight='balanced'. Finds the maximum-margin hyperplane between mood classes. Class balancing corrects for the uneven distribution in our target variable.

KNN (k=6) — Best Model

Classifies by majority vote via Euclidean distance. We tuned k from 1 to 20. k=6 gave the best test accuracy at 64.3% with the smallest train/test gap of any model.

Linear Regression — Baseline

Included to test whether mood could be predicted as a linear function. R squared of negative 0.24 said no, which confirmed classifiers were the right approach.

Calibrated SVM

CalibratedClassifierCV wrapping SVM via isotonic regression with 5-fold CV. Adds probability scores so predictions feel interpretable rather than just a class label.

Decision Tree — Feature Selection

Two experiments: all 11 features versus screen-time features only. Gini impurity revealed which variables the model actually relied on, which led to our most important finding.

KNN Hyperparameter Tuning

Before locking in our KNN model, we tested every value of k from 1 to 20 to find the sweet spot between underfitting and overfitting. At k=1 the model memorizes the training data perfectly, giving 100% training accuracy but very poor test performance. As k increases, training accuracy drops and test accuracy stabilizes.

Fig 4. KNN accuracy vs. k for values 1 to 20. k=6 gives the best balance between training and test performance, with test accuracy of 64.3% and training accuracy of 58.9%.

Why k=6 stood out: It was the only point where test accuracy reliably exceeded training accuracy. That means the model is genuinely generalizing to new data rather than just memorizing the training set. For a dataset this small, that matters a lot.

The Calibrated SVM trades some Good mood accuracy for better Neutral coverage. It catches 2 Neutral samples correctly and manages 5 Good, but routes the Bad sample to Neutral. The calibration adds useful probability scores even where the raw predictions are the same as the uncalibrated SVM.

Feature Importance: The Finding That Changed How I Think About This

This is the part that mattered most to me personally. We trained a Decision Tree on all 11 features and got 57.1% test accuracy. Then we stripped it down to screen-time features only (Social Media Hours and Video Game Hours) and it dropped to 42.9%. That is a 15-percentage-point drop just from removing the psychological variables.

But the importance chart tells an even more specific story. Look at what the model actually relied on most:

Fig 9. Feature importance scores from the Decision Tree (Gini impurity). Using for Relief ranks highest, followed by Video Game Hours and Social Comparison. Raw screen time hours matter, but not as much as why and how people use those screens.

What this chart actually says: "Using for Relief" is the single most predictive feature. People who use screens specifically to escape or cope with stress are the ones whose mood suffers most. This is not about how long you are online. It is about what you are doing online and why. The psychological relationship with technology matters more than the hours.

What We Concluded

1

KNN Was Our Best Model

64.3% test accuracy with the smallest train/test gap. The only model where test accuracy exceeded training accuracy, which is the clearest sign of genuine generalization.

2

Avoidance Coping Is the Key Signal

"Using for Relief" was the highest-ranked feature. People using screens to escape stress report worse mood outcomes. The data confirms what psychology research has been saying for years.

3

Psychological Factors Beat Raw Hours

Removing screen-time-only features dropped accuracy by 15 points. Social comparison, sleep disruption, and avoidance coping predict mood better than hours online.

4

Mood Is Non-Linear

R squared of negative 0.24 from Linear Regression confirmed mood cannot be predicted as a straight-line function of screen behavior. Classifiers were the right tool.

5

Calibration Makes Predictions Useful

Probability scores per class make predictions interpretable in a way that a single label cannot. Bad 3%, Good 54%, Neutral 44% is more actionable than just saying "Good."

What This Means in Practice

Most advice around screen time focuses on limits: set a timer, put your phone down, use an app. But our data suggests that framing misses the point. Two people can spend the same number of hours online and have completely different mood outcomes depending on whether those hours come from connection or avoidance, from curiosity or from trying to numb something out.

What I take from this personally: I do not think I will start tracking my screen time differently after this project. But I will probably be more honest with myself about whether I am picking up my phone because I want to or because I am avoiding something. That feels like a more useful question than the number of hours, and the data backs it up.

Limitations, Ethics and Reflection

Small Sample70 rows, mostly UNCC students. We cannot claim these findings generalize beyond our immediate community.
Self-Reported BiasRespondents may misremember screen time or mood at the time they filled out the survey.
Class ImbalanceOnly 1 Bad sample in the test set. No model could reliably detect this class, and that is a real gap in what we can conclude.
Correlation vs. CausationAcademic stress or other factors may independently drive both screen use and lower mood at the same time.
PrivacyAll responses were fully anonymous. No personally identifiable information was collected at any point.
Not a Clinical ToolThis is for awareness and education only. It should never be used for diagnosis or individual mental health screening.
My personal reflection: What surprised me was not that the model worked. It was what made it work. I went in expecting screen time hours to dominate. Instead "Using for Relief" carried the most weight. That felt true in a way that accuracy numbers alone cannot capture, and it is the part of this project I will still be thinking about long after the semester ends.

Conclusion

This started as a class project and became something I genuinely cared about. We built our own dataset, trained five models, and found that the way people use screens matters far more than how long they use them.

For a self-collected survey with 70 respondents, that is a real and meaningful finding. It is not a clinical conclusion. But it points in a direction worth following up on with a larger, more diverse sample in the future.
