Screen Time and Mental Health

Why This Project Matters to Me

Mental health is something I have personally seen become more talked about, but still not fully understood, especially among college students. At UNC Charlotte, I have noticed how common it is for students, including myself sometimes, to spend hours on social media or gaming either to relax or to escape from stress.

What stood out to me is that the impact of this usage is not the same for everyone. Some people feel better after using these platforms, while others feel more anxious, lonely, or drained. That difference is what made this project meaningful to me.

Research supports this complexity too. The Pew Research Center has reported that social media can increase pressure and anxiety among teens, while sources like Psychology Today highlight that online platforms can also provide connection and support. Reports summarized by Engadget indicate that around 37% of teens report sleep disruption from platforms like TikTok.

Our research question: How accurately can hours of social media and gaming usage, combined with self-reported psychological responses, predict the day-to-day mental health outcomes (daily mood) of individuals? We wanted to move beyond simple assumptions and use machine learning to detect real patterns in how behavior and psychological responses together relate to mood.

Why We Collected Our Own Data

My teammates Nik, Ojas, and I all had the same intuition going in. We did not think raw screen hours were the real story. We thought the why mattered more than the how long. We could not find an existing dataset that captured both behavioral screen habits and the psychological side-effects we cared about, so we built one ourselves.

We distributed a Google Form survey across UNC Charlotte and the broader Charlotte community in April 2026 and collected 100 responses. After cleaning, 70 were usable.

What We Measured

After cleaning, we had 70 usable rows and 11 features. The target variable was Daily Mood, rated 0 to 4 by each respondent and binned into three classes.

  • Social Media Hours - daily time on social platforms, ordinal
  • Video Game Hours - daily gaming time, ordinal
  • Anxiety Levels - self-reported anxiety linked to screen use
  • Social Comparison - tendency to compare oneself to others online
  • Self Esteem - self-reported rating
  • Screen Use for Relief - whether screens are used to cope with stress
  • Loneliness - feelings of isolation linked to screen use
  • Sleep Affected - whether screen use disrupts sleep
  • Addiction Level - self-assessed compulsive use
  • Gamer Status - whether the respondent identifies as a gamer

Target variable: Daily Mood rated 0 to 4, binned as Bad (0 to 1), Neutral (2), and Good (3 to 4).

Worth naming upfront: With 70 rows and 14 test samples, every percentage point of accuracy represents about 0.14 samples. We treated our results as directional signals rather than definitive conclusions, and tried to be honest about that throughout.

  • Applied StandardScaler for KNN, fit on training data only to prevent leakage

The Five Models We Trained

Support Vector Machine

RBF kernel with class_weight='balanced'. Finds the maximum-margin hyperplane between mood classes. Class balancing corrects for the uneven mood distribution in our data.

KNN — Best Model (k=6)

Classifies by majority vote via Euclidean distance. We tuned k from 1 to 20 and found k=6 gave the best test accuracy at 64.3% with the smallest gap between training and test performance.

Linear Regression — Baseline

We included this to test whether mood could be predicted as a simple linear function of screen behavior. An R squared of negative 0.24 said no, which confirmed we needed classifiers.

Calibrated SVM

CalibratedClassifierCV wrapping SVM via isotonic regression with 5-fold CV. Gives probability scores per class so predictions feel more interpretable and uncertainty-aware.

Decision Tree — Feature Selection

Two experiments: all 11 features versus screen-time features only. Gini impurity ranked which features the model actually relied on, which led to our most important finding.

The result that surprised me: KNN was the only model where test accuracy exceeded training accuracy, 64.3% versus 58.9%. That is the smallest train/test gap we saw and the clearest sign of genuine generalization rather than the model just memorizing the training data.

The Finding That Changed How I Think About This

This is the part that mattered most to me personally. We trained a Decision Tree on all 11 features and got 57.1% test accuracy. Then we stripped it down to screen-time features only, Social Media Hours and Video Game Hours, and it dropped to 42.9%. That is a 15-percentage-point drop just from removing the psychological variables.

What the Gini importance scores revealed: Anxiety, Addiction Level, Sleep Affected, and Loneliness ranked higher in feature importance than raw screen hours. The quality of your relationship with technology predicts your mood better than how long you are online. Screen time is a symptom. The psychological experience is the cause.

What We Concluded

1

KNN Was Our Best Model

64.3% test accuracy with the smallest train/test gap. The strongest evidence of real generalization among everything we tested.

2

Psychological Factors Beat Screen Time

Anxiety, sleep disruption, addiction feelings, and loneliness were stronger predictors of mood than raw hours online. This was our core finding and the one that felt most true.

3

Avoidance Coping Backfires

People who used screens specifically to escape stress reported lower moods overall. Using technology to avoid your problems does not work, and the data shows it.

4

Mood Is Non-Linear

R squared of negative 0.24 confirmed mood cannot be predicted as a straight-line function of screen behavior. Classifiers were the right approach.

5

Calibration Makes Predictions Useful

Probability scores like Bad 3%, Good 54%, Neutral 44% make a prediction feel real and interpretable rather than just a label.

What This Means in Practice

Most conversations about screen time focus on limits: set a timer, use an app, put your phone down. But our data suggests that framing misses the point. Two people can spend the same number of hours online and have completely different mood outcomes depending on whether those hours come from connection or avoidance, from curiosity or from trying to numb something.

If you want to understand how your screen use is affecting your mood, the question is not how long you are online. The question is why you keep going back and how you feel when you stop.

What I take from this personally: I do not think I will start tracking my screen time differently after this project. But I will probably be more honest with myself about whether I am picking up my phone because I want to or because I am avoiding something. That feels like a more useful question than the number of hours.

Limitations, Ethics and Reflection

Small Sample70 rows, mostly UNCC students. We cannot claim these findings generalize beyond our community.
Self-Reported BiasRespondents may misremember screen time or mood at the time they completed the survey.
Class ImbalanceOnly 1 Bad sample in the test set. No model could reliably detect this class, which is a real limitation.
Correlation vs. CausationAcademic stress or other factors may independently drive both screen use and lower mood at the same time.
PrivacyAll responses were fully anonymous. No personally identifiable information was collected at any point.
Not a Clinical ToolThis is for awareness only. It should never be used for diagnosis or individual mental health screening.
My personal reflection: What surprised me was not that the model worked. It was what made it work. I went in expecting screen time hours to dominate. Instead anxiety and avoidance coping carried the weight. That felt true in a way the accuracy numbers alone cannot capture, and it is the part of this project I will still be thinking about long after the semester ends.

Conclusion

This started as a class project and became something I genuinely cared about. We built our own dataset, trained five models, and found that the way people use screens matters far more than how long they use them.

For a self-collected survey with 70 respondents, that is a real and meaningful finding. It is not a clinical conclusion. But it points in a direction worth following up on with a larger, more diverse sample in the future.
