When Screen Time Meets Mental Health

Can AI detect mood signals from how we use social media and gaming? We built our own dataset to find out — and the answer surprised us.

The Question That Started This

Most of us spend hours each day on social media and video games without really thinking about what that does to how we feel. The conversation usually reduces to a single metric — screen time — as if hours logged is all that matters. But is that really what drives mood? Or is it something deeper about why we're online and how it makes us feel?

This project set out to answer that. We collected our own survey data from UNC Charlotte students and the broader Charlotte community, then trained machine learning models to classify daily mood as Bad, Neutral, or Good from behavioral and psychological features.

Research Questions: Does screen time predict daily mood? Does gaming reduce or increase stress? What factors actually matter most — raw hours, or the psychological side-effects of screen use? And can AI detect mental health signals from self-reported behavioral data?

The Dataset

We self-collected data using a Google Form survey distributed to UNC Charlotte students and the Charlotte, NC community in April 2026. After removing incomplete responses and cleaning erroneous entries, we had 70 usable rows with 11 features.

  • Social Media Hours — daily time on social platforms (ordinal)
  • Video Game Hours — daily gaming time (ordinal)
  • Anxiety Levels — self-reported anxiety related to screen use
  • Social Comparison — tendency to compare self to others online
  • Self Esteem — self-reported self-esteem rating
  • Screen Use for Relief — using screens to cope with stress
  • Loneliness — feelings of loneliness associated with screen use
  • Sleep Affected — whether screen use disrupts sleep
  • Addiction Level — self-assessed compulsive use pattern
  • Gamer Status — whether the respondent identifies as a gamer

Target variable: Daily Mood — rated 0–4, then binned into three classes: Bad (0–1), Neutral (2), and Good (3–4).

Acknowledged limitation: With only 70 usable rows and 14 test samples, every percentage point represents roughly 0.14 samples. Results should be treated as directional signals, not definitive benchmarks.

Models & Why We Chose Them

Five models were selected to cover a range from interpretable baselines to probability-aware classifiers, each revealing a different aspect of the data.

📐 Support Vector Machine (SVM)

RBF kernel with class_weight='balanced'. Finds the maximum-margin hyperplane between mood classes. Minimizes ½‖w‖² subject to yᵢ(w·xᵢ+b) ≥ 1. Class balancing corrects for skewed mood distribution.

🏆 KNN — Best Model (k=6)

Classifies by majority vote among k nearest training samples via Euclidean distance. Features normalized with StandardScaler. k tuned from 1–20; k=6 was optimal with 64.3% test accuracy and smallest overfitting gap.

📉 Linear Regression — Baseline

Tests whether mood can be predicted as a continuous linear function. R²=−0.24 confirmed it cannot, establishing a meaningful lower bound and validating the classifier approach.

🎯 Calibrated SVM

CalibratedClassifierCV wraps SVM via isotonic regression (5-fold CV). Produces reliable probability scores per class — e.g., Good 54%, Neutral 44%, Bad 3% — enabling uncertainty-aware predictions.

🌳 Decision Tree — Feature Selection

Two experiments: trained on all 11 features vs. screen-time features only. Gini impurity ranks feature importance — psychological variables consistently outranked raw screen hours.

The key story in the numbers: KNN was the only model where test accuracy exceeded training accuracy (64.3% vs 58.9%), indicating genuine generalization rather than memorization. The Decision Tree hit 76.8% training accuracy but only 57.1% on test — a clear sign of overfitting on a small dataset.

The Feature Importance Finding

The most revealing experiment wasn't about overall accuracy — it was the feature selection test. We trained a Decision Tree on all 11 features (57.1% test accuracy), then retrained it using only screen-time features — Social Media Hours and Video Game Hours (42.9%). That's a 15-percentage-point drop from removing the psychological variables.

What the Gini scores revealed: Anxiety, Addiction Level, Sleep Affected, and Loneliness ranked higher in feature importance than raw screen hours. The quality of your relationship with technology predicts mood better than how long you're online. Screen time is a symptom — not the cause.

Key Findings

1

KNN (k=6) Was the Best Model

64.3% test accuracy with the smallest train-test gap — the strongest evidence of genuine generalization among all five models tested.

2

Psychological Factors Beat Screen Time

Anxiety, sleep disruption, addiction feelings, and loneliness were stronger predictors of daily mood than hours spent on social media or gaming.

3

Avoidance Coping Backfires

Respondents who used screens to escape stress reported lower moods — consistent with established maladaptive coping theory in psychology.

4

Mood Is Non-Linear

R²=−0.24 from Linear Regression confirms mood cannot be predicted as a linear function of screen behavior, validating the classifier approach.

5

Calibration Adds Interpretability

Probability scores (Bad 3%, Good 54%, Neutral 44%) make predictions transparent and uncertainty-aware — important for any sensitive application.

Why This Matters Beyond the Classroom

The standard conversation about screen time and mental health focuses on duration — how many hours is too many? But this project suggests that's the wrong question. Models with access to psychological features consistently outperformed those with only raw screen time.

That distinction matters practically. Screen-time limits are easy to measure but may address the symptom rather than the cause. If someone spends hours online because they feel lonely or anxious, the screen time isn't the driver — the underlying psychological state is.

The real takeaway: The question for your mental health isn't "how long are you online?" — it's "why are you going online, and how does it make you feel?" These are harder to measure but far more predictive.

Limitations, Ethics & Reflection

Small SampleOnly 70 usable rows, mostly UNCC students. Results should be treated as directional, not definitive.
Self-Reported BiasRespondents may misremember screen time or their mood at the time they completed the survey.
Class ImbalanceOnly 1 "Bad" sample in the test set. No model could reliably detect this class — false negatives are invisible here.
Correlation ≠ CausationAcademic stress or other confounders may independently drive both high screen use and low mood.
PrivacyAll survey responses were fully anonymous. No personally identifiable information was collected at any point.
Not a Clinical ToolFor awareness and education only — never use for diagnosis or individual mental health screening.
Personal reflection: What surprised me most wasn't that the model worked — it was what made it work. Going in, I expected screen time hours to be the dominant signal. Instead, the psychological features carried the weight. The way technology affects us isn't just about how much we use it — it's about the relationship we have with it.

Conclusion

This project started with a simple question — can AI detect mental health signals from screen behavior? — and arrived at a more nuanced answer. Yes, patterns exist in this kind of data. But the patterns that matter most aren't the ones you'd measure with a screen-time tracker.

Anxiety, loneliness, sleep disruption, and compulsive use consistently outranked raw hours as predictors of daily mood. For a project built on self-collected survey data with 70 respondents, that's a real and meaningful finding worth following up on with larger, more diverse datasets.
