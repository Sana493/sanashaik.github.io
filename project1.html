Problem Definition

Sleep plays a critical role in physical health, mental well-being, productivity, and long-term disease prevention. Yet many people struggle to get enough sleep due to stress, work schedules, lifestyle habits, or environmental factors.

This project explores four key questions:

  • How much do people sleep on average?
  • Does sleep duration vary by occupation?
  • Is there a relationship between stress level and sleep duration?
  • What patterns exist between lifestyle factors and sleep?
Why it matters: Sleep affects nearly every aspect of daily life. Students, healthcare workers, and professionals in high-stress fields may especially benefit from understanding how stress and occupation influence their sleep patterns — and what levers they have to change them.

The Dataset

The dataset used is the Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset from Kaggle. Each row represents one individual and includes demographic, occupational, and physiological data.

  • Gender — male or female
  • Age — in years
  • Occupation — job category
  • Sleep Duration — hours of sleep per night
  • Quality of Sleep — self-rated 1–10
  • Stress Level — self-rated 1–10
  • Physical Activity Level — minutes per day
  • BMI Category — underweight / normal / overweight / obese
  • Heart Rate — resting beats per minute
  • Daily Steps — step count

The dataset contains just over 300 records — small but manageable for exploratory analysis.

Dataset limitations: Unknown data collection method, unknown country or demographic background, likely self-reported values. Results reflect patterns within this dataset and cannot be generalized to the broader population.

Data Cleaning & Preparation

Before analysis, several cleaning steps were performed to ensure data quality and consistency:

  • Checked for missing values — none found requiring removal
  • Checked for and removed duplicate rows
  • Verified correct data types across all columns
  • Ensured numeric columns were properly formatted for analysis
  • Confirmed categorical columns (Occupation, BMI Category) were consistent

No large data removal was required, preserving the full 300+ record dataset for analysis.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Sleep Duration Distribution

A histogram of sleep duration reveals that most individuals in the dataset sleep between 6 and 7 hours per night, with the 6.5–7 hour range being most common. Very few respondents reported sleeping fewer than 6 or more than 8 hours regularly.

Fig 1. Distribution of sleep duration across all 300+ respondents. The distribution is approximately normal, centered around 6.5–7 hours.

Sleep Duration by Occupation

When broken down by occupation, meaningful variation emerges. Some professions cluster tightly around specific sleep ranges, while others show wide spread — suggesting that the relationship between work and sleep differs significantly by field.

Fig 2. Sleep duration by occupation. Doctors and engineers show the widest range, while other occupations display more consistent sleep patterns.

Stress Level vs. Sleep Duration

The clearest pattern in the dataset: a strong negative relationship between stress level and sleep duration. As reported stress increases, average sleep duration decreases — a relationship that holds across occupations and age groups.

Fig 3. Stress level vs. sleep duration. The negative trend is consistent — higher self-reported stress is reliably associated with shorter sleep.

The key pattern: Stress is the strongest single predictor of sleep duration in this dataset. Higher stress → shorter sleep, across occupations, genders, and age groups. This holds even after accounting for physical activity levels.

Key Findings

Average Sleep
Most respondents sleep 6–7 hours nightly. The 6.5–7 hour range is the single most common window in the dataset.
Occupation Matters
Doctors and engineers show the most variable sleep, suggesting high-demand professions create unpredictable schedules.
Stress Is the Driver
The stress–sleep relationship is the strongest signal: higher stress levels consistently predict shorter sleep duration.

Why This Matters Beyond the Classroom

Sleep research typically requires expensive lab equipment and longitudinal tracking. What this project demonstrates is that even a modest self-reported survey dataset can surface meaningful, actionable patterns — particularly around the stress-sleep relationship.

For students, understanding that stress management is a direct lever for sleep improvement is practically useful. For researchers, this kind of exploratory analysis suggests which variables are worth investigating more rigorously with larger, controlled datasets.

Limitations, Ethics & Reflection

  • Small sample size: 300+ records is enough for exploration but too small for generalizable conclusions
  • Self-reported bias: Sleep duration and stress level are both self-reported and may not reflect objective measurements
  • No demographic context: Unknown country, culture, or population background limits applicability
  • Missing variables: Work hours, screen time, caffeine intake, and room environment are likely confounders not captured here
  • Cross-sectional: A single snapshot in time — no longitudinal tracking of how these patterns evolve
What I'd explore next: If I had more time, I would investigate sleep quality (not just duration), compare patterns across age groups, and build a regression model to quantify the marginal impact of each variable on sleep duration while controlling for the others.
