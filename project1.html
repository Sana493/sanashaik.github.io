📊 Sleep Health & Lifestyle Analysis

A Data Science Exploration by Sana Shaik

1. Problem Definition

Sleep plays a critical role in physical health, mental well‑being, productivity, and long‑term disease prevention. Yet many people struggle to get enough sleep due to stress, work schedules, lifestyle habits, or environmental factors.

This project explores four key questions:

  • How much do people sleep on average?
  • Does sleep duration vary by occupation?
  • Is there a relationship between stress level and sleep duration?
  • What patterns exist between lifestyle factors and sleep?

These questions matter because sleep affects nearly every aspect of daily life. Students, healthcare workers, and professionals in high‑stress fields may especially benefit from understanding how stress and occupation influence sleep patterns.

2. Data Description

The dataset used is the Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset from Kaggle. Each row represents one individual and includes:

  • Gender
  • Age
  • Occupation
  • Sleep Duration (hours)
  • Quality of Sleep
  • Stress Level
  • Physical Activity Level
  • BMI Category
  • Heart Rate
  • Daily Steps

The dataset contains a little over 300 records, making it small but manageable for exploratory analysis.

Limitations

  • Unknown data collection method
  • Unknown country or demographic background
  • Likely self‑reported values
  • Cannot be generalized to the entire population

3. Data Cleaning & Preparation

Before analysis, several cleaning steps were performed:

  • Checked for missing values
  • Checked for duplicate rows
  • Verified correct data types
  • Ensured numeric columns were properly formatted
  • Confirmed categorical columns were consistent

There were no major missing values, so no large data removal was required.

4. Exploratory Data Analysis

Sleep Duration Distribution

A histogram revealed that most individuals sleep between 6 and 7 hours, with 6.5–7 hours being the most common.

Sleep Duration by Occupation

Doctors and engineers have the widest range of sleep duration, while some occupations show more consistent patterns.

Stress Level vs Sleep Duration

A clear negative relationship exists: higher stress levels are associated with shorter sleep duration.

5. Storytelling & Interpretation

The strongest insight is the stress–sleep relationship: higher stress levels are consistently associated with shorter sleep duration. Occupation also influences sleep patterns, though less strongly.

However, correlation does not imply causation. Other factors may be involved.

6. Limitations, Ethics & Reflection

  • Small sample size
  • Self‑reported values
  • No demographic or geographic context
  • Missing variables like work hours or screen time

If I had more time, I would explore sleep quality, compare age groups, and build a regression model.
