Why I Chose This Topic

As a college student, sleep is something I genuinely think about. Between classes, assignments, and everything else, getting enough rest feels like a constant trade-off. I wanted to bring actual data to that experience and find out what the numbers say about what drives sleep patterns.

The four questions I wanted to answer:

  • How much do people actually sleep on average?
  • Does your occupation affect how long you sleep?
  • Is there a real connection between stress and sleep duration?
  • What other lifestyle factors show up in the data?
Why this matters to me personally: I see how stress and schedule pressure affect sleep around me every day at UNCC. I wanted to know whether the data confirms what I was already observing, and whether it could point to anything actionable.

The Dataset

I used the Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset from Kaggle, covering just over 300 individuals with data on their demographics, occupations, and health metrics.

  • Gender, Age, Occupation
  • Sleep Duration in hours per night
  • Quality of Sleep self-rated 1 to 10
  • Stress Level self-rated 1 to 10
  • Physical Activity Level in minutes per day
  • BMI Category, Heart Rate, Daily Steps
Honest limitation: The data collection method is unknown, the country is unclear, and the values are self-reported. I kept these constraints in mind and did not try to draw conclusions beyond what the data can actually support.

Cleaning and Preparation

Before doing any analysis I ran through the standard checks: missing values, duplicates, data types, and column consistency. There were no major issues, so all 300+ records stayed in.

  • No missing values requiring removal
  • No duplicate rows found
  • Numeric and categorical column formatting verified throughout

What I Found

Sleep Duration Distribution

Most people in this dataset sleep between 6 and 7 hours, with 6.5 to 7 being the single most common range. Very few reported consistently sleeping under 6 or over 8 hours.

Fig 1. Sleep duration across all respondents. The distribution is roughly normal, centered around 6.5 to 7 hours per night.

Sleep by Occupation

When I broke sleep down by occupation, real differences appeared. Some fields cluster tightly around specific sleep windows while others show wide spread. Doctors and engineers had the most variation, which makes sense given how unpredictable those schedules tend to be.

Fig 2. Sleep duration grouped by occupation. Higher-demand professions tend to show more variation in how much sleep people get.

Stress vs. Sleep

This was the finding that stuck with me. Higher stress consistently predicts shorter sleep across the entire dataset. It held across different occupations and age groups, which suggests it is a genuine pattern rather than noise.

Fig 3. Stress level vs. sleep duration. The negative trend is steady and consistent throughout the data.

The finding I keep thinking about: Stress is the single strongest predictor of sleep duration in this dataset. Not your job, not your age. Stress. That tells me that managing what stresses you out is probably more impactful for your sleep than adjusting your schedule alone.

Key Takeaways

Average Sleep
Most people sleep 6 to 7 hours. The 6.5 to 7 hour window is the most common range in the dataset.
Occupation Matters
High-demand jobs like medicine and engineering create the most unpredictable sleep patterns.
Stress Is the Driver
The strongest predictor of shorter sleep is higher stress, and it holds across every subgroup I looked at.

Limitations and Reflection

  • Small sample: 300+ records is enough for exploration but too small for broad conclusions
  • Self-reported values: Both stress and sleep duration depend on respondents accurately reporting their own experience
  • No demographic context: Unknown country and population limits how far I can apply the findings
  • Missing variables: Screen time, caffeine, work hours, and sleep environment are likely factors not captured here
What I would explore next: I want to look at sleep quality separately from duration, compare patterns across age groups, and build a regression model to measure the independent effect of each variable while controlling for everything else.
