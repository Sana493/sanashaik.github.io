Project 1 – Sleep Health & Lifestyle Analysis

Exploring how stress, occupation, and lifestyle relate to sleep

← Back to Home

1. Problem Definition

Sleep is essential for both physical and mental health, yet many people struggle to get enough rest due to stress, work demands, or lifestyle habits. In this project, I explored several questions:

  • How much do people sleep on average?
  • Does sleep duration vary by occupation?
  • Is there a relationship between stress level and sleep duration?
  • What patterns exist between lifestyle factors and sleep?

These questions matter because sleep affects productivity, mood, heart health, and overall well‑being. Students, workers, and healthcare professionals may care about understanding how stress and occupation influence sleep habits.

2. Data Description

The dataset comes from Kaggle and is called the Sleep Health and Lifestyle Dataset. Each row represents one individual and includes:

  • Gender, Age, Occupation
  • Sleep Duration (hours)
  • Quality of Sleep
  • Stress Level
  • Physical Activity Level
  • BMI Category
  • Heart Rate
  • Daily Steps

The dataset contains a little over 300 records. One limitation is that we do not know how the data was collected or from which country, so results cannot be generalized broadly.

3. Data Cleaning & Preparation

I performed several cleaning steps:

  • Checked for missing values
  • Checked for duplicate rows
  • Verified numeric and categorical data types
  • Ensured sleep duration and stress level were numeric

There were no major missing values. I assumed the data was self‑reported and recorded correctly.

4. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Sleep Duration Distribution: Most people sleep between 6–7 hours, with 6.5–7 being the most common.

Sleep by Occupation: Doctors and engineers show wider variation; some occupations have slightly lower median sleep.

Stress vs. Sleep: A clear negative relationship — higher stress is associated with shorter sleep duration.

Correlation Heatmap: Sleep duration negatively correlates with stress; physical activity and steps are positively correlated.

5. Storytelling & Interpretation

The strongest pattern is the relationship between stress and sleep. Higher stress levels are linked to shorter sleep duration. Occupation also plays a role, though the patterns are less strong. Most people in the dataset sleep around 6–7 hours.

However, correlation does not imply causation. Many other factors — work hours, lifestyle habits, or health conditions — could influence these patterns.

6. Limitations, Ethics & Reflection

Key limitations include:

  • Small sample size
  • Unknown data collection method
  • Likely self‑reported values
  • No demographic or geographic context

I was surprised by how consistent sleep duration was across the dataset. If I had more time, I would explore sleep quality, age groups, and build a simple regression model.
