When the Most Important Data Disappears

Diabetes has been part of my family for as long as I can remember. This project came from a question I could not stop thinking about: what happens when the most critical data point is simply not there?

The Question That Started This

Diabetes has been part of my family for as long as I can remember. I have watched the daily routines up close: the careful meals, the glucose checks, the way everything seemed to hinge on one number. Blood sugar.

That observation turned into a question I could not let go of. In real healthcare settings, data goes missing all the time. A patient might not have had a recent glucose test. Records get lost. Tests do not get run. So if a machine learning model completely depends on glucose to work, is it actually useful in the real world?

My research question: When glucose is removed from the model, can machine learning still make meaningful predictions about diabetes risk? And what features step in to carry the weight?

The Dataset

I used the Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset from Kaggle and the UCI ML Repository. It contains 768 patient records from Pima Indian women, each with 8 clinical measurements and a binary outcome: diabetic or not.

  • Pregnancies - number of pregnancies
  • Glucose - plasma glucose concentration
  • Blood Pressure - diastolic blood pressure in mm Hg
  • Skin Thickness - triceps skinfold thickness in mm
  • Insulin - 2-hour serum insulin
  • BMI - body mass index
  • Diabetes Pedigree Function - a score reflecting family history
  • Age

While exploring the data I noticed that several features had zero values in places where zero is medically impossible. These were placeholders for missing data, not real measurements, and I treated them accordingly before modeling.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Fig 1. Feature distributions after preprocessing. Insulin and Skin Thickness are heavily right-skewed. The Outcome histogram reveals a 65/35 class split, which matters for how we interpret accuracy. A model that always predicts "no diabetes" would still be 65% accurate.

Fig 2. Correlation heatmap. Glucose correlates with the outcome at 0.49, nearly double BMI at 0.31 and Age at 0.24. This confirmed exactly what I expected before running a single experiment.

What the heatmap told me early on: When glucose is removed, the model will lean on BMI, Age, and Diabetes Pedigree Function next. The structure of substitution is visible in the correlations before any experiment starts.

Preprocessing

I replaced the biologically impossible zeros in Glucose, Blood Pressure, Skin Thickness, Insulin, and BMI with the column median from valid non-zero entries. I chose median over mean because features like Insulin are heavily right-skewed, and the mean would be pulled toward extreme values in a way that does not represent a typical patient.

  • 80/20 train/test split with stratification to preserve the 65/35 class balance
  • StandardScaler applied for Logistic Regression, fit on training data only to prevent data leakage
  • Tree-based models used raw unscaled values since they are scale-invariant by design
  • Random seed fixed at 42 throughout for reproducibility
One thing I would do differently: For Insulin especially, the zeroed values are so common that median imputation may not reflect real physiology. KNN imputation, which uses relationships between features rather than a single population value, would be stronger in a follow-up study.

Models and Why I Chose Them

  • Logistic Regression - the interpretable linear baseline. If glucose is the dominant linear signal, removing it should hurt this model the most, and it did.
  • Decision Tree - captures non-linear patterns by splitting the feature space into regions. I left it unconstrained intentionally to observe the overfitting behavior.
  • Random Forest (200 trees) - the ensemble approach. More robust to noise, redistributes weight across features naturally, and provides built-in importance scores that are central to answering my research question.

The Four Experiments

I ran four feature configurations across all three models, progressively removing predictive power to observe how each model degrades and what it falls back on.

Experiment 1: All Features

The performance ceiling. Confirms feature dominance before any removals.

Experiment 2: Remove Glucose

The core question. What is left when the dominant signal disappears?

Experiment 3: Remove Glucose, BMI and Age

The three highest-correlated features gone. Only moderate and weak signals remain.

Experiment 4: Weak Features Only

The absolute floor. Can any real signal survive in just the low-correlation variables?

What the Results Showed

Fig 3. Accuracy across all four experiments. The decline is gradual, not sudden. Random Forest never falls below roughly 67%. Logistic Regression shows the sharpest drop from Experiment 1 to Experiment 2.

The result that answered my question: Removing glucose hurts, but it does not break the model. Random Forest dropped about 5 percentage points and then held steady. That gradual degradation instead of collapse is the whole point of the project. These models have real flexibility and are not entirely dependent on a single feature.
Logistic Regression
Strong with all features. Most sensitive to glucose removal since its linear decision boundary depends heavily on that one signal.
Decision Tree
Moderate and inconsistent. Performance fluctuates across experiments, likely because of overfitting without depth constraints.
Random Forest
Best overall. Most robust to feature removal. The model I would recommend for a real deployment scenario.

The Part That Really Bothered Me: False Negatives

Accuracy is one way to measure a model. In healthcare, how you are wrong matters just as much as how often. The confusion matrices below show what happens to Logistic Regression as features disappear, and the pattern is uncomfortable.

Exp 1. 82 true negatives, 27 true positives. 18 false negatives.

Exp 2. 80 true negatives, 25 true positives. 29 false negatives.

Exp 3. 85 true negatives, 21 true positives. 33 false negatives.

Exp 4. 85 true negatives, 21 true positives. 33 false negatives.

What the climb from 18 to 33 actually means: As features disappear, the model does not just become less accurate overall. It becomes specifically worse at catching diabetic patients. A false negative means a diabetic person gets told they are fine. That is not a statistical error. That is a real health outcome for a real person. This is the finding I kept coming back to throughout the whole project.

Feature Importance: What the Model Leans On

Random Forest reveals its reasoning through feature importance scores, measuring how much each variable reduces prediction error across all 200 trees.

Fig 5. With all features, Glucose accounts for roughly 27% of the model's decision-making weight. Nearly double what BMI contributes at 15%.

Fig 6. Without Glucose, BMI rises to 21%, followed by Diabetes Pedigree Function and Age. The model does not collapse. It redistributes.

Why the redistribution matters: BMI reflects metabolic health. Diabetes Pedigree Function captures inherited genetic risk. Age reflects cumulative physiological exposure over a lifetime. Each captures a different dimension of diabetes risk. Together they tell a different story than glucose does, but still a meaningful one.

Limitations, Ethics and What I Take From This

  • Population specificity: This dataset covers only Pima Indian women. Diabetes risk factors differ across ethnicities, genders, and regions. This model should never be applied to a broader population without revalidation.
  • Imputation assumptions: Replacing zeros with medians is reasonable but it is still an assumption, not a real measurement.
  • Class imbalance: The 65/35 split was not corrected with resampling or class weighting, which likely suppresses recall for diabetic cases.
  • No hyperparameter tuning: The Decision Tree was left unconstrained, making its overfitting expected and intentional for this study.
  • Ethical use: These are research tools. Predicting that someone likely has diabetes should never replace a clinical evaluation by a real doctor.
What stayed with me: Watching the false negatives climb from 18 to 33 made the whole project feel personal in a way the accuracy numbers alone never could. Behind each of those 33 predictions is a person who would be told they are fine when they are not. That is the reason I care about getting this right, and the reason this project connected to something real for me.

Conclusion

This started as a prediction task and became something more personal. By deliberately removing the most important feature I could see how these models adapt, what they fall back on, and exactly where they start to fail.

The core finding: even when the most important data disappears, patterns remain. The model does not know glucose is gone. It just works with what it has. And in this dataset, what is left is still enough to say something meaningful about diabetes risk.

That connects back to what I grew up observing. Diabetes is not just one number. It is family history, body composition, age, and years of physiology building up together. The data reflects that complexity, and so does the model.
