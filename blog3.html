Social Determinants of Health & The Capabilities Approach

By Sana Shaik

← Back to Home

The Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) describe the conditions that shape a person’s daily life—things like housing, education, transportation, income, neighborhood safety, and access to healthcare. These factors influence far more than medical outcomes; they shape a person’s opportunities, stress levels, and long-term well-being. For example, two people with the same illness may have very different health outcomes depending on whether they have stable housing, reliable transportation to appointments, or access to nutritious food.

The Capabilities Approach, developed by Amartya Sen and expanded by Martha Nussbaum, focuses on what people are actually able to do and be. Instead of measuring success only through income or economic growth, the Capabilities Approach asks whether people have the real freedom to live a life they value. For instance, education is not just a statistic—it is a capability that expands a person’s ability to participate in society, pursue meaningful work, and make informed decisions.

These two concepts connect because the Social Determinants of Health directly shape a person’s capabilities. If someone grows up in a neighborhood with underfunded schools, limited job opportunities, and unsafe housing, their capabilities are restricted long before they make any personal choices. On the other hand, supportive environments—safe communities, good schools, accessible healthcare—expand a person’s capabilities and allow them to thrive. In this way, SDOH can be seen as the structural foundation that either enables or limits human capabilities.

To understand these concepts in real-world terms, we might measure variables such as educational attainment, income stability, access to healthcare, transportation availability, neighborhood crime rates, or environmental quality. These indicators help reveal how opportunity is distributed and where inequalities emerge. For example, mapping access to grocery stores or public transit can show how certain communities face barriers that others do not.

Thinking about health and well-being through both SDOH and the Capabilities Approach encourages us to look beyond individual choices and consider the broader systems that shape people’s lives. It reminds us that well-being is not just about personal responsibility—it is about whether society provides the conditions that allow everyone to flourish.
