How Opportunities Shape Health

How social conditions and human capabilities shape real-life well-being — and what the data can and can't tell us.

Health Is Not Just Biology

When we think about health, we tend to think about the body — genetics, diet, exercise, medical care. But decades of public health research point to something broader: the conditions in which people are born, grow, work, and age are among the most powerful predictors of health outcomes, often more powerful than the clinical factors we tend to focus on.

These are called the social determinants of health — and they include things like income, education, housing stability, neighborhood environment, and access to healthy food. None of these appear on a blood panel, but all of them shape what ends up there.

The core insight: A person's zip code is often a better predictor of their health outcomes than their genetic code. The conditions that shape what opportunities people have access to — stable work, safe housing, quality schools — shape their bodies over time in ways that show up as health or illness decades later.

The Capabilities Approach

Philosopher and economist Amartya Sen developed a framework called the capabilities approach that offers a useful lens here. Rather than measuring well-being by income or consumption, it asks: what are people actually able to do and be?

A person might have adequate income but live in a neighborhood without grocery stores, breathe polluted air, work a job with no sick leave, and experience chronic stress from housing insecurity. By income metrics they might look fine. By capabilities metrics — what they can actually do, how they can actually live — the picture looks very different.

This matters for data science because the variables we choose to measure, and the outcomes we choose to predict, embed assumptions about what well-being means. If we only measure what's easy to quantify, we systematically miss the harder-to-measure factors that often matter most.

What Data Can Tell Us

Large-scale health datasets have revealed striking patterns in how social conditions map onto health outcomes. Neighborhoods with higher rates of poverty show higher rates of nearly every chronic disease. Educational attainment predicts life expectancy with uncomfortable precision. The relationship between income inequality and population health is robust across dozens of countries and study designs.

These patterns are real and important. They tell us that if we want to improve population health, we need to look beyond clinical interventions — at the social, economic, and environmental conditions that make people sick in the first place.

What data can't tell us: Correlation is not mechanism. Knowing that poverty correlates with poor health outcomes doesn't tell us exactly how that relationship works, which interventions would most effectively interrupt it, or how to weigh the trade-offs between different approaches. The data points to where to look — it doesn't tell us what to do.

Why This Connects to My Work

The projects I've worked on — predicting diabetes risk, analyzing sleep patterns — sit at the intersection of individual biology and social conditions. The Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset reflects a population with historically high rates of diabetes, driven in part by decades of displacement, food insecurity, and disrupted traditional diets. The sleep dataset reflects patterns shaped by stress, occupation, and lifestyle factors that are themselves shaped by socioeconomic position.

A model that predicts diabetes risk without accounting for the social conditions that drive that risk might be technically accurate but practically misleading — because it locates the problem in the individual when the causes are often structural.

That doesn't mean we shouldn't build these models. It means we should be honest about what they can and can't see. And it means the question "what causes this?" requires a much wider lens than any single dataset can provide.

What I take away: The most interesting questions in health data science sit at the boundary between what's measurable and what matters. Getting good at this work means being fluent in both — the technical tools for working with data, and the broader understanding of why the patterns in that data look the way they do.
