How Opportunities Shape Health

On social determinants, what the data can and cannot tell us, and why where you grow up matters more than most models account for.

Health Is Not Just Biology

When we talk about health, we tend to focus on the body: genetics, diet, exercise, medical care. But decades of public health research point to something broader. The conditions in which people are born, grow, work, and age are among the most powerful predictors of health outcomes, often more powerful than the clinical factors we usually center in the conversation.

These are called the social determinants of health. They include income, education, housing stability, neighborhood environment, and access to healthy food. None of them show up on a blood panel. All of them shape what eventually does.

Something that has stuck with me: A person's zip code is often a better predictor of their health outcomes than their genetic code. The conditions that shape what opportunities people have access to, stable work, safe housing, quality schools, shape their bodies over time in ways that show up as health or illness decades later.

The Capabilities Approach

Philosopher and economist Amartya Sen developed a framework called the capabilities approach that offers a useful lens here. Rather than measuring well-being by income or consumption, it asks: what are people actually able to do and be?

A person might have adequate income but live in a food desert, breathe polluted air, work a job with no sick leave, and carry the chronic stress of housing uncertainty. By income metrics they might look fine. By capabilities metrics, what they can actually do and how they can actually live, the picture looks very different. This matters for data science because the variables we choose to measure embed assumptions about what well-being means. If we only measure what is easy to quantify, we systematically miss the harder-to-measure factors that often matter most.

What Data Can Tell Us

Large-scale health datasets have revealed striking patterns in how social conditions map onto health outcomes. Neighborhoods with higher poverty rates show higher rates of nearly every chronic disease. Educational attainment predicts life expectancy with uncomfortable precision. The relationship between income inequality and population health holds across dozens of countries and study designs.

These patterns are real and important. They tell us that if we want to improve population health meaningfully, we need to look beyond clinical interventions and at the social, economic, and environmental conditions that make people sick in the first place.

What data cannot tell us: Correlation is not mechanism. Knowing that poverty correlates with poor health outcomes does not tell us how that relationship actually works, which interventions would interrupt it most effectively, or how to weigh the trade-offs between different approaches. The data points to where to look. It does not tell us what to do when we get there.

Why This Connects to My Own Work

I grew up in India and moved here in 2022, and I watched my hometown navigate the kinds of economic pressures that the research describes. Healthcare access, the weight of financial uncertainty on daily decisions, the gap between what people need and what systems can actually provide. These are not abstractions to me. They shaped how I think about what data should actually be useful for and who it should serve.

The projects I have worked on sit at this intersection. The Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset reflects a population shaped by decades of displacement and food insecurity. The sleep dataset reflects patterns driven by stress and occupation that are themselves shaped by socioeconomic position. A model that predicts outcomes without acknowledging what caused them upstream might be technically accurate but practically misleading, because it locates the problem in the individual when the causes are often structural.

That does not mean we should not build these models. It means we should be honest about what they can and cannot see, and what questions they can and cannot answer on their own.

What I take away: The most interesting questions in health data science sit at the boundary between what is measurable and what matters. Getting good at this work means being fluent in both: the technical tools for working with data, and the broader understanding of why the patterns in that data look the way they do. You need both to do the work responsibly.
